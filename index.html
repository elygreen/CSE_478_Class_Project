The story of the Colorado River and its current challenges

The lifeline for 40 million people in the American West, the Colorado River is not just a landscape but a critical resource under threat. This is the story of how we got here and what lies ahead. For decades, the river has brought life into the desert, supporting cities, agriculture, and ecosystems, but the era of abundance is ending. After two decades of drought and overallocation, the system is at a breaking point. This presentation explores the history, current state, and future projections.

Instead, it uses a complex combination of different source to meet its water needs: Snowmelt, Colorado River water, reclaimed wastewater, and groundwater all play a role. Colorado River Law Allocation

The "Law of the River" is a complex set of agreements, compacts, and court decisions that dictate how the Colorado River's water is divided among the seven basin states and Mexico. The law recognizes the effect that drought has on water availability and includes provisions for reducing allocations during times of shortage.

Note that Arizona is among the top users of Colorado River water, receiving 2.8 million acre-feet (MAF) annually under normal conditions.

  • California: 4.4 MAF
  • Arizona: 2.8 MAF
  • Nevada: 0.3 MAF

Lake Mead Storage (1980-2025)

Lake Mead is the main "checking account" for the Lower Basin states. As levels drop, the risk of having to deplete more of our water reserves increases. We are currently hovering dangerously close to thresholds where water delivery becomes physically difficult.

Lake Powell's Declining Reservoir

Upstream, Lake Powell acts as the system's shock absorber, trapping snowmelt from the Rockies to regulate flow to the Lower Basin. However, with prolonged drought and increased water demand, Lake Powell's levels have plummeted as well, and the dam struggles to maintain its critical functions.

The Invisible Crisis: Groundwater

While the river shrinks, a quieter emergency is unfolding beneath us. As surface water dries up, agriculture and cities turn to groundwater, leading to alarming rates of depletion. Our whole reliability on the Colorado River was supposed to move us away from being reliant on groundwater, a resource that takes thousands of years to replenish. Yet, as the river's flow diminishes, we are pumping our groundwater faster than nature can put it back, permanently draining our last emergency fund.

The End of the Beginning

The era of cheap, abundant water is over. The Colorado River, once a symbol of prosperity and growth, is shrinkinkng and our aquifers are dropping. Securing the future of the American West will reuire more than just efficiency and luck, it now requires a fundamental rethinking of how we live in the desert. The choices we make in these upcoming years will define this lanscape for generations to come.
